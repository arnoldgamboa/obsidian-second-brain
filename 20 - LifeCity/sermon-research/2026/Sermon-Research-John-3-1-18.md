# Sermon Research: John 3:1-18

Nicodemus and the New Birth

---

## Step 1: Passage Context

**Book overview.** The Gospel of John was written by John, the son of Zebedee, one of the twelve disciples and the "beloved disciple." Unlike the Synoptic Gospels (Matthew, Mark, Luke), John does not follow a strict chronological order of events. Instead, he selects seven "signs" and long discourses to build a theological case that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God. Most scholars place the writing of John between AD 80-95, decades after the other Gospels, from Ephesus. By then, the church had separated from synagogue worship, and John writes both to strengthen believers and to call unbelievers to faith.

**Audience.** The Gospel was written for a mixed audience: Jewish Christians who were being expelled from synagogues (see John 9:22), Gentile believers, and seekers. John assumes knowledge of Jewish customs but also explains them for outsiders. The readers would have known the Pharisees, understood the significance of the Temple, and recognized the tension between Jesus and the Jewish authorities. They also lived in a world where "eternal life" was a contested concept between Jewish expectation and Greco-Roman philosophy.

**Literary genre.** John is a Gospel, a unique literary form that blends historical narrative with theological reflection. It is not a biography in the modern sense. John selects material for its theological weight. The passage is a discourse: a nighttime conversation that functions as a dramatic scene with theological density. The structure is dialogue (Nicodemus speaks, Jesus responds), followed by monologue (Jesus explaining the new birth and the Son of Man's lifting up), followed by a summary reflection from the narrator (verses 16-18).

**Placement in the book.** This passage comes early in John, immediately after Jesus' first sign at the wedding in Cana and the cleansing of the Temple. It is the first extended teaching of Jesus in the Gospel. It sets up the central theme of John: how one enters the kingdom of God. What follows in the Gospel is a series of encounters (the Samaritan woman, the healing at Bethesda, the feeding of the five thousand) that all circle back to this question: who is Jesus, and what does it mean to believe in Him? This passage is the theological foundation for everything that follows.

## Step 2: Historical and Cultural Background

**Political and social realities.** Judea in the first century was under Roman occupation. The Jewish people maintained religious autonomy in some areas, but Rome controlled the political and military power. The Sanhedrin, the Jewish ruling council, had limited authority. Nicodemus is a member of this council (verse 1), which means he is part of the religious elite. He comes to Jesus "at night" (verse 2), a detail that matters. Night in John's Gospel often symbolizes spiritual darkness or secrecy. ==Nicodemus comes under cover of darkness because associating with Jesus publicly would risk his position and reputation.

**Religious context.** The Pharisees were one of the major Jewish sects, known for their strict observance of the Torah and the oral tradition. They believed in the resurrection of the dead, angels, and the coming Messiah. Nicodemus calls Jesus "Rabbi" (verse 2) and acknowledges that God is with Him because of the signs. This is significant: ==Nicodemus is not hostile.== He is curious, perhaps even convinced. But his category for Jesus is still "teacher sent from God." He ==does not yet understand that Jesus is the Son of God.==

The phrase =="born again" (or "born from above") ==would have been shocking to Nicodemus. Jewish proselytes were sometimes described as having a new life after conversion, but the idea that a Jewish man, a Pharisee, a leader of Israel, would need to be born again would have been offensive. Nicodemus takes it literally (verse 4) because the idea is so radical that it almost demands a literal misreading before the spiritual meaning can break through.

**Cultural practices.** Rabbinic dialogue often involved a teacher and a student using riddles, questions, and surprising statements to push the student into deeper understanding. Jesus does not answer Nicodemus directly. He answers a question Nicodemus has not asked. Nicodemus opens with a statement of admiration. ==Jesus responds with a demand for rebirth. ==This is classic Johannine technique: ==Jesus exposes the real need beneath the surface conversation.==

**What modern readers miss.** Three details a contemporary reader would likely pass over:

1. The significance of Nicodemus being a "teacher of Israel" (verse 10). Jesus tells him that he, of all people, should understand this. ==The one trained in the Torah, the one who studies the prophets, the one who teaches others, is the one who is spiritually blind==. This is not just irony. ==It is a claim that formal religious education cannot replace spiritual rebirth==.

2. "Water and Spirit" (verse 5). Modern readers often split these into baptism and the Holy Spirit, or into different aspects of conversion. But in the Jewish context, water was associated with ritual cleansing, especially the ==mikvah==, the ritual bath for purification. "Water and Spirit" echoes Ezekiel 36:25-27, where God promises to sprinkle clean water and put a new spirit within His people. ==Jesus is not inventing a new ritual. He is claiming to be the fulfillment of Israel's deepest longing for cleansing and renewa==l.

3. "The Son of Man must be lifted up" (verse 14). This alludes to Numbers 21, where Moses lifted up the bronze serpent so that the Israelites, bitten by poisonous snakes, could look at it and live. ==The serpent was a symbol of judgment that became a means of salvation.== Jesus draws a direct parallel: He must be lifted up (crucified) so that those dying in sin can look at Him and live. A modern reader might miss that "lifted up" in John is a double meaning: ==lifted up on the cross, and lifted up in exaltation.==

## Step 4: Commentary Insights

**D.A. Carson** (The Gospel According to John): Carson emphasizes that "from above" (anothen) is the primary meaning, with "again" as a secondary nuance. Nicodemus hears "again" and misses "from above." Carson argues that verse 5 is not about Christian baptism (a later church practice) but about the eschatological renewal promised in Ezekiel 36. ==Water and Spirit together describe one act of regeneration==, not two separate requirements. Carson also stresses that verse 16 is not a generic promise but a specific explanation of how the new birth happens: through belief in the only begotten Son.

**Craig Keener** (The Gospel of John: A Commentary): Keener draws extensively on first-century Jewish sources to show that "born again" was not a common concept in mainstream Judaism, though some Jewish texts spoke of converts as "newborn children." Keener notes that Nicodemus represents the educated elite who should have understood the prophets but is spiritually blind. Keener also highlights the irony: Nicodemus comes at night, misunderstands, and leaves apparently unconvinced. ==Yet he reappears twice in John (7:50-52 defending Jesus, and 19:39 bringing spices for Jesus' burial), suggesting a journey of faith.==

**Leon Morris** (The Gospel According to John): Morris focuses on the absolute necessity of the new birth. "Unless" (ean me) in verse 3 and verse 5 is strong: without this, there is no entry into the kingdom. Morris sees John 3:16 as the pivot of the passage and perhaps the entire Gospel. He argues that "eternal life" is not just quantity of life but quality: the life of God entering human existence.

**Frederick Dale Bruner** (The Gospel of John: A Commentary): Bruner reads this passage as a story of conversion. ==Nicodemus thinks he has arrived (he is a Pharisee, a ruler, a teacher). Jesus tells him he has not even begun.== Bruner calls this "the theology of the absolute beginning." No amount of ==religious achievement can substitute for the new birth==. Bruner also emphasizes the Trinitarian structure of the passage: the Spirit (verse 5-8), the Son (verse 13-15), and the Father's love (verse 16).

**N.T. Wright** (John for Everyone): Wright frames this passage around the question of how God's kingdom comes. ==For Nicodemus, it comes through righteousness and Torah observance. For Jesus, it comes through a radical new beginning that only God can give==. Wright connects the bronze serpent to the cross: just as the serpent was a symbol of judgment that became salvation, so the cross is the place where sin and death are dealt with, and life is offered.

**Where commentators agree:**
- ==Nicodemus is sincere but spiritually blind.==
- The new birth is entirely God's work, not human effort.
- "Water and Spirit" refers to one act of divine cleansing and renewal, not two separate requirements.
- John 3:16 is the theological heart of the passage.

**Where they diverge:**
- Whether "born of water" refers to Christian baptism, Jewish ritual cleansing, or natural birth. Most recent commentators reject the baptism view as anachronistic.
- Whether Nicodemus is converted in this passage or is on a longer journey. His later appearances suggest the latter.

## Step 6: Theological Themes

### 1. The Necessity of the New Birth

**How it appears in the text:** Jesus uses the strongest possible language: "Unless one is born again, he cannot see the kingdom of God" (verse 3). "Unless" means absolutely necessary, not optional, not helpful, not recommended. Without this, there is no entry.

**Practical implication:** Your congregation includes people who have attended church for decades, who know the Bible, who serve on committees, who teach Sunday school, and who have never been born again. Religious activity cannot replace spiritual rebirth. Nicodemus is the warning: the most religious person in the room may be the one who needs the new birth most.

### 2. The Sovereignty of the Spirit

**How it appears in the text:** "The wind blows where it wishes, and you hear its sound, but you do not know where it comes from or where it goes. So it is with everyone who is born of the Spirit" (verse 8). The Spirit is not controlled, manipulated, or scheduled. He moves according to His own will.

**Practical implication:** Many Christians treat the Spirit like a resource to be tapped when they need power, comfort, or guidance. Jesus says the Spirit is sovereign. This means we cannot manufacture revival. We cannot program spiritual transformation. We can pray, preach, and plead, but the new birth is the Spirit's work, not ours. This is humbling for pastors and liberating for the anxious.

### 3. The Cross as the Means of Salvation

**How it appears in the text:** "As Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, so must the Son of Man be lifted up, that whoever believes in Him may have eternal life" (verses 14-15). The cross is not an accident, a tragedy, or a failure. It is a divine necessity: "so must."

**Practical implication:** Some preachers avoid the cross because it sounds negative, bloody, or old-fashioned. Jesus points to it as the center of salvation. The new birth is not a self-improvement program. It is a rescue operation. The cross is where the rescue happens. Preach it plainly. Do not apologize for it.

### 4. The Love of God in Sending the Son

**How it appears in the text:** "For God so loved the world, that He gave His only Son, that whoever believes in Him should not perish but have eternal life" (verse 16). This is not just a verse for children's church. It is the explanation of how the new birth is possible. God loved. God gave. We believe. We live.

**Practical implication:** Many people in your congregation struggle to believe that God loves them. They know He loves the world in general. They are not sure He loves them specifically. This verse says "whoever." The offer is universal, but the application is personal. Preach it to the person who feels excluded, unworthy, or beyond hope.

### 5. Judgment and Belief

**How it appears in the text:** "Whoever believes in Him is not condemned, but whoever does not believe is condemned already, because he has not believed in the name of the only Son of God" (verse 18). Belief is the dividing line. Not moral performance, not religious background, not good intentions. Belief.

**Practical implication:** Your congregation needs clarity, not confusion, about what saves. Jesus does not say, "Whoever tries hard is not condemned." He does not say, "Whoever is sincere is not condemned." He says, "Whoever believes." This is exclusive in its means (only through Jesus) and inclusive in its offer (whoever believes). Preach both edges of that sword.

## Step 7: Thinking Prompts

1. Nicodemus is a religious leader who is sincere, educated, and morally upright, but Jesus tells him he needs to start over. Who in your congregation most needs to hear that religious achievement cannot substitute for the new birth? Are you tempted to soften this because it might offend the committed members?

2. Jesus does not explain how the new birth happens before He demands it. "You must be born again" comes before the explanation of the cross. Are you preaching the demand of the Gospel before the mechanism, or are you so eager to explain that you never confront?

3. The Spirit is compared to wind: unseen, sovereign, unpredictable. How does this challenge your desire to control outcomes, measure success, or claim credit for conversions?

4. John 3:16 is the most memorized verse in the Bible, and possibly the most trivialized. How do you preach it so that it lands with the person who has heard it a thousand times but never believed it?

5. Nicodemus leaves the conversation apparently unconvinced, yet he reappears twice in John's Gospel. Are you judging the success of your preaching by immediate responses? What would it look like to preach for the long journey of faith, not just the instant decision?

6. The cross is described as a necessity: "so must the Son of Man be lifted up." Do you preach the cross as essential, or do you treat it as optional or embarrassing? What would change in your preaching if you believed the cross was the only means of salvation?

7. Jesus tells Nicodemus, a teacher of Israel, that he should have known these things. What blind spots might you have as a pastor, teacher, or leader? Where might you be the Nicodemus in the room: educated, respected, but missing the most basic spiritual reality?
