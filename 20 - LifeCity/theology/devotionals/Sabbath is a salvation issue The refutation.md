I've seen old arguments here that presents a logical progression to show that the Sabbath is a salvation issue. Sometimes I feel irritated, sometimes I smile, sometimes I feel helpless (as I don't have time to write the refutation). Well, I have 20 minutes now.

Most Adventist believe that Sabbath is a salvation issue -- ie. if you don't observe the sabbath, you're going to hell, and variants of that. (Some Adventists who are progressives, some even my classmates who are now pastors, do not think this way. If you see some of them, please extend a hand of fellowship to them, my fellow evangelicals)

To prove that Sabbath is a salvation issue, they will present a logical  progression like this;

Is having other gods a salvation issue? Yes.
Is murder a salvation issue? Yes.
Is adultery a salvation issue? Yes
All these are part of the 10 Commandments. The Sabbath is part of the 10 Commandments, therefore, Sabbath is a salvation issue.

2 WAYS TO REFUTE THIS ARGUMENTATION:

1. LOGICAL FALLACY REFUTATION

If a logical progression is fallacious, it shouldn't be considered at all in the first place. That's very basic, not just for those of us who took Logic 101 in college but also for those who want to have a health discussion.

This logical progression that they are presenting is fallacious. This is called the "Fallacy of Division". It is committed by inferences from the fact that a whole has a property to the conclusion that a part of the whole also has that property.

(1) Water is liquid.
Therefore:
(2) H2O molecules are liquid.

This argument, in attributing a macro-property of water, liquidity, to its constituent parts, commits the fallacy of division. Though water is liquid, individual molecules are not.

Same is true with the 10 Commandments. While the 10 Commandments is moral (in fact, some commandments not even part of the 10Cs has moral components), you cannot immediately assume that all parts of it is moral. That's a false assumption, therefore, the fallacy of division.

If it's a fallacious logical progression, it should be dismissed right away. They should use another way to prove that Sabbath is a salvation issue.

2. FALSE ASSUMPTIONS

To be able to prove that this argumentation is correct, there needs to have really huge assumptions :

This assumes:

a. That the Sabbath law is moral in the same value as the morality of murder. 

This is a wrong assumption and can be proven my just looking at how morality is established in different societies. 

God created us in his image. Whether you are a theist or not, he placed some sort of moral compass built in our being. One major test of morality is by how we think and feel when we do a certain act. Even if you know the 10 Commandments or not, you know for a fact that murder is wrong. That stealing is wrong. That sleeping with somebody else's wife is wrong. But has anyone actually felt it is wrong to NOT observe the Sabbath on Saturday, or even to observe the Sabbath at all? I've seen someone felt really bad for having sex with someone not his wife, and he's not even a Christian. But I've never seen someone felt so bad for not going to church on Saturday! By that, you should really think whether the Sabbath is indeed moral in the same breath as murder, adultery and the rest.

b. That the Sabbath is moral because the rest of the 9 in the 10 commandments are moral.

Connected with the logical fallacy refutation, this assumption is false. The other 9 is moral, yes. But obviously, the 4th commandment is not (see previous assumption), it is ceremonial. And God has a purpose why he placed a ceremonial law in the middle of the 10 commandments. 

Deut. 5:2,3 says, "The Lord our God made a covenant with us at Horeb. It was not with our ancestors that the Lord made this covenant, but with us". He wants to identify the 10 commandments with his people the Israel, that's why he used the Sabbath as the sign of that covenant. Exe. 20:12,20 (the Adventist pet verse, btw) explains this when God said: "Keep my Sabbaths holy, that they may be a sign between us."

Therefore, the Sabbath has a purpose inside the moral 10 commandments. It is to be a sign between God and the nation of Israel.  But that doesn't mean it's moral, because well, it is ceremonial (sign).

c. That the gospel -- Jesus' incarnation, life, death and resurrection -- boils down to whether you'll obey the whole of the 10 commandments or not, with special emphasis to the Sabbath commandment.

There are 3 major theological frameworks that concerns this:

- Covenant Theology (CT), which includes the Adventists and the Reformed churches like most Baptists, Presbyterians, etc. These churches believe that the 10 Commandments still exists today.

- Dispensationalism 

- New Covenant Theology, more like a bridge between the CT and Disp. This group believes that there is a new covenant and it doesn't include the 10 commandments but includes a HIGHER set of laws that Jesus gave. I belong to this theological framework.

The CTs (Reformed churches that support the 10 commandments and the Sabbath, whether Saturday (Seventh-da Baptists) or Sunday (Presbys, etc) ) doesn't think that Sabbath, while we need to obverse it, affects your status in heaven. To them, salvation is all about the grace of God. We are sinners, we repent of our sins, accept Christ as our savior and be covered by his blood. He is our passport to heaven, not our obedience to the Sabbath commandment.

Not so with their fellow CT, the Seventh-day Adventist Church. They believe that if you disregard the Sabbath law, you'll go down in hell.

If that is the case, Christ's blood is so cheap. As Paul said in Galatians, Christ died in vain. Even if you believe that Christ has shed his blood for you, but you don't believe that the Sabbath needs to be observed by Christians, you're going to hell and his blood has no effect whatsoever. It's all up to you. Even if you truly love God, serve him and others, but don't observe the Sabbath the way Adventists wants us to believe, then all of it amounts to nothing.

Christianity and the gospel, then, is all about the Sabbath?

I refuse to let people be deceived by this falsehood. The gospel is so wonderful, it changes the lives of people. It changed mine. It changed the trajectory of my life. Have you ever heard someone said, Oh the Sabbath changed my life! No. I haven't. Because the gospel of God's grace changes lives, not the Sabbath. 

Don't let this argumentation fool you. Don't let others be fooled by this, too. Live the gospel. Teach the gospel. Defend the gospel.
