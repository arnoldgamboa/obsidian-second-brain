### LinkedIn post text

Most AI agent projects do not fail because the model is too weak.

They fail because the handoff is vague.

A small business owner says, “I want an AI employee to handle this,” but the real workflow is full of hidden decisions:

- Which customer requests are routine?
- Which ones need human judgment?
- What data source is trusted?
- What counts as “done”?
- When should the AI stop and ask?

If those rules live only in someone’s head, automation just makes the ambiguity faster.

The practical starting point is not “which model should we use?”

It is:

1. Write down the current workflow.
2. Mark the repeatable decisions.
3. Mark the judgment calls.
4. Define the escalation points.
5. Give the AI one narrow lane to own.

That is less exciting than a demo.

But it is how an AI assistant becomes useful instead of impressive for five minutes.

For operators, the scar-tissue lesson is simple: before you hire an AI employee, design the job description.

### LinkedIn post text

A useful AI employee is usually boring.

It does not need to sound futuristic.
It does not need to replace a department.
It does not need to impress LinkedIn.

It needs to reliably remove friction from work that already happens every week.

For a small business, that might mean:

- Turning call notes into follow-up tasks
- Drafting first-pass replies for routine inquiries
- Checking whether a client file is complete
- Summarizing support themes before the weekly meeting
- Preparing a manager with the context they need before a decision

None of that is glamorous.

But if it saves a few hours, reduces dropped balls, and gives the owner more confidence that the machine is not making silent mistakes, that is real value.

The temptation is to start with the flashiest use case.

The better move is often to start with the workflow everyone is tired of repeating.

Boring is not the opposite of valuable.

In operations, boring is often where the margin is.

### LinkedIn post text

Not every workflow should be fully automated.

That is not anti-AI. It is just operational maturity.

Some work carries customer trust, brand risk, financial consequences, or emotional context. In those workflows, the best AI assistant may not be the one that “does everything.”

It may be the one that prepares the human to make a better decision.

That can still be powerful:

- Gather the relevant history
- Summarize the customer’s situation
- Highlight missing information
- Draft a recommended response
- Flag risks and edge cases
- Create the follow-up task after approval

The human keeps the judgment.
The AI removes the drag around the judgment.

That distinction matters.

A lot of businesses do not need a fully autonomous agent on day one. They need a dependable assistant that makes the next human action clearer, faster, and better informed.

Automate the preparation.
Protect the judgment.
Earn trust one workflow at a time.
