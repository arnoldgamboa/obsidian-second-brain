The work that hurts an operation is not always the work that is visibly late.

Sometimes it is the work everybody assumes somebody else owns.

I have learned to look for that gap before I get excited about an AI agent. A lead gets handed from marketing to sales. A client request moves from inbox to project tool. A meeting ends, but the decision, owner, and deadline live in three different people’s memory.

Nothing is technically broken. Until it is.

Then the customer follows up, the owner gets dragged in, and the team spends an hour reconstructing a handoff that should have been clear in the first place.

That is a good use case for an **AI agent for workflow handoffs**.

Not an agent that makes promises or reassigns work blindly. Not another dashboard the team has to remember to check. I mean a narrow operating layer that watches the handoff points you already use and asks practical questions:

- Did the next owner actually receive the work?
- Is the required context attached?
- Is there a committed next step and date?
- Has the item sat untouched past the team’s normal window?
- Does a human need to decide what happens next?

The key word is *watches*.

Too many AI projects begin by giving the system authority before the business has earned confidence in its visibility. That creates a polished version of the same old problem: the agent did something, but now nobody knows why.

I would rather start by making the handoff legible.

A useful AI employee can collect the thread, CRM record, meeting note, task status, and customer history into one short handoff summary. It can flag missing inputs. It can draft the follow-up or create a proposed task for review. It should stop before changing scope, committing money, or sending a customer-facing message without a clear rule.

The tradeoff is obvious: this is not the dramatic “AI runs operations” story. It is slower to earn the autonomy headline.

But it is also how you avoid building a second management job.

When the team can see exactly where work changes hands—and where it quietly disappears—you get cleaner accountability before you get clever automation. That is usually the better order.
