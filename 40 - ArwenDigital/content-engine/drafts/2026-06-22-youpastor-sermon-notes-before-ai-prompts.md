I am increasingly convinced that the first question pastors should ask about AI is not, “Can it write this sermon?”

A better question is: “Can it help me work with the notes I already have?”

That difference matters.

A blank AI prompt invites the tool to lead. It asks the machine to decide the angle, structure, emphasis, and application before the pastor has done the slower work of observation, prayer, and judgment. Sometimes the output sounds impressive. That is part of the danger. A polished outline can make thin work feel finished.

But most pastors do not sit down with nothing. They have half-formed notes from reading the passage. They have old study material. They have a burden from a hospital visit, a counseling conversation, a small group discussion, or the quiet sense that this text needs to land carefully this week.

Those notes may be messy, but they are not worthless. They are where pastoral context begins.

This is where I think AI can be genuinely useful. Not as a sermon generator, but as a sermon notes assistant.

For example, I would rather ask AI to:

- group my observations without adding new claims
- point out where my outline feels unclear
- identify applications that sound generic
- turn my rough notes into questions I still need to answer
- suggest communication follow-ups after I approve the sermon direction

That workflow keeps the pastor in the lead. The tool is responding to actual pastoral work instead of inventing a sermon-shaped object from scratch.

There is still a tradeoff. AI can organize notes faster than I can, but it can also flatten them. It can make everything sound smoother and less local. That means the pastor still has to review, delete, rewrite, and sometimes ignore the suggestion entirely.

That is not a bug in the process. That is the process.

This is one reason I am building YouPastor around workflows, not just prompts. A pastor should be able to bring real notes, keep context, approve major steps, and carry the sermon into the rest of the week without handing over the responsibility that belongs to them.

If AI helps pastors do that, I am interested.

If it teaches pastors to skip their own notes, I am not.
