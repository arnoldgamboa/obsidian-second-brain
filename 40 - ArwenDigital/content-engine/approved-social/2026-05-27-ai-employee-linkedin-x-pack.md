### LinkedIn post text

```text
The best first AI employee is usually not the impressive one.

It is not the agent that “runs the whole business.”
It is not the demo that books meetings, writes proposals, updates the CRM, creates invoices, and posts on social media in one magical loop.

That kind of scope sounds exciting.
It also breaks in boring, expensive ways.

The better first workflow is usually something painfully specific:

- every new lead gets summarized and assigned
- every sales call produces next steps
- every Friday report gets pulled and explained
- every support inbox gets triaged before the team opens it
- every stale CRM deal gets a follow-up draft

Small enough to inspect.
Repeated enough to matter.
Clear enough to know when it worked.

This is one of the biggest tradeoffs I keep seeing with AI agents:

If you start with a huge promise, the agent becomes a science project.
If you start with one boring workflow, the agent becomes an employee.

That first workflow teaches you the real operating system:

What data does the agent need?
What permissions are safe?
Where should it pause for approval?
What does “done well” actually mean?
Who reviews the output?

Once that loop works, expanding is much easier.

My bias now: don’t ask, “What can AI automate in my company?”

Ask, “What is one recurring workflow I would gladly delegate every week if I trusted the handoff?”

That is the better starting point.
```

### LinkedIn post text

```text
Your dashboard is probably already an AI employee job description.

Not because the dashboard is useless.
Because of what happens after you open it.

Most operators do not just “check metrics.”

They do a quiet sequence of judgment calls:

- open the dashboard
- compare this week to last week
- notice what moved
- decide what matters
- ignore the noise
- write the summary
- assign the follow-up
- remind someone before it gets forgotten

That is work.

And a lot of it is repeatable.

If a human logs into Shopify, Search Console, a CRM, Stripe, or a reporting tool every week and turns numbers into “here is what changed and here is what we should do,” that is not just reporting.

That is an analyst workflow.

An AI employee does not need to replace the operator’s judgment to be useful here.

It can do the first pass:

- pull the numbers
- compare against the previous period
- flag anomalies
- draft the plain-English summary
- suggest follow-up questions
- prepare the update before the meeting

Then the human reviews, corrects, and decides.

That is the deployment pattern I trust more than flashy “agent runs the business” demos.

Give the agent a recurring surface area.
Give it clear permissions.
Give it a review gate.
Let it remove the repetitive checking and first-draft analysis.

If you are trying to find your first AI employee use case, look at the dashboard you keep opening.

The workflow may already be sitting there.
```
