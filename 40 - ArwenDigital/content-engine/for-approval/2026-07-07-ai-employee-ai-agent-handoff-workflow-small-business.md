# Your AI Agent Should Own the Handoff, Not the Whole Department

I do not think most small businesses should start by asking an AI agent to run a department.

That sounds obvious until you watch the demos.

The agent reads the inbox, updates the CRM, drafts proposals, schedules meetings, writes reports, and follows up with customers. It looks like a tiny operations team living inside a browser.

But inside a real business, that is usually too much surface area for day one.

The better first question is smaller:

Where does work already get dropped between two steps?

That is the handoff.

And in my experience, handoffs are where a first AI employee can become useful without becoming dangerous.

## Departments are too vague

“Help with sales” is not a workflow.

“Handle operations” is not a workflow.

“Take care of admin” is not a workflow.

Those are departments, categories, and buckets of pain. They may describe the pressure you feel, but they do not give an agent a job it can reliably complete.

A handoff is different.

A new lead fills out a form, and someone needs to research the company before the call.

A sales call ends, and someone needs to turn notes into a recap, next steps, and CRM updates.

A customer sends a support request, and someone needs to classify it, gather context, and draft the first response.

A weekly report is due, and someone needs to pull numbers from three places before the owner can make a decision.

Those are narrow enough to inspect. Repeated enough to matter. Clear enough to improve.

That is the kind of work I would rather give a first AI agent.

## The handoff reveals the real operating rules

The hidden benefit of starting with a handoff is that it forces the business to say what good work looks like.

What information should the agent read?

What systems can it update?

What should it draft but not send?

When does it escalate to a human?

What tone should it use with customers?

What mistakes would be annoying, and what mistakes would be expensive?

These questions sound basic, but they are the difference between an agent demo and an agent deployment.

Most businesses do not fail with AI because the model cannot write a paragraph. They fail because nobody defined the operating lane. The agent gets a broad instruction, touches too many systems, and creates a new kind of management work for the team.

That is why I like handoffs. They make the boundaries visible.

## A good first agent reduces drag, not judgment

The goal is not to remove the operator from every decision.

The goal is to remove the drag around the decision.

If an AI agent can gather the account history, summarize the last call, draft a follow-up, and flag the one unclear point, the owner still decides what to send. But the owner is no longer starting from scattered tabs and memory.

That is real leverage.

Not because the agent became the sales manager.

Because the agent made the next human decision faster, cleaner, and easier to trust.

That is also why I am careful with the word autonomy. Autonomy is useful only after the workflow is understood. Before that, more autonomy just means faster confusion.

A managed AI employee should earn scope the same way a human employee does: by doing one job well, showing its work, and proving that the review loop is healthy.

## Start where the work already leaks

If you are considering AI agents for your business, I would not start with the flashiest process.

I would look for the handoff that repeatedly costs time:

- lead received → researched and assigned
- call finished → recap and next steps drafted
- email received → classified and routed
- proposal requested → first draft prepared
- report due → numbers gathered and explained
- invoice overdue → follow-up drafted for review

That is usually where the first agent belongs.

Small enough to supervise. Useful enough to matter. Boring enough to survive real operations.

And once that handoff works, you have something better than an AI experiment.

You have the beginning of an operating system.
