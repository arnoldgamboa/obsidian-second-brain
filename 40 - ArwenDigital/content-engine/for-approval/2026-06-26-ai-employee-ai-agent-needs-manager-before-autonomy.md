# Your AI Agent Needs a Manager Before It Needs More Autonomy

I think a lot of businesses are asking the wrong question about AI agents.

They ask, “How autonomous can this be?”

The better first question is, “Who is going to manage it?”

That sounds less exciting. It also sounds closer to how real operations work.

A new employee does not become useful because you give them unlimited freedom on day one. They become useful because someone gives them a clear job, shows them where the information lives, defines what good work looks like, and checks the first few outputs before trusting them with more.

AI agents are not that different.

## Autonomy without management creates hidden work

I have a strong bias against the “set it and forget it” version of AI automation.

Not because agents cannot do useful work. They can. But unmanaged agents create a strange kind of operational debt.

They summarize the wrong thread. They update the wrong field. They draft a follow-up that sounds almost right but misses the actual customer concern. They make quiet assumptions because nobody defined the escalation rule.

Then the operator has to investigate what happened, rebuild trust, and add manual checks after the fact.

That is not leverage. That is a new workflow wearing an automation costume.

## A managed agent has a job description

The businesses that will get value from AI agents first are not the ones chasing full autonomy.

They are the ones willing to define the work clearly:

- What triggers the agent?
- What sources can it use?
- What should it draft, update, or prepare?
- What decisions require approval?
- What should it never touch?
- Who reviews exceptions?
- What does success look like every week?

This is why I keep framing AI agents as digital employees, not tools.

A tool waits for someone to figure it out. An employee needs management. A good managed agent sits somewhere in between: it owns a repeatable workflow, but it still has guardrails, review points, and someone improving it.

## The real service is the operating loop

The model matters. The prompts matter. The integrations matter.

But the operating loop matters more.

Someone has to watch the outputs, tighten the instructions, add missing context, remove risky permissions, and decide when the agent is ready for more responsibility.

That is the part many businesses underestimate. They do not just need an AI agent. They need an agent manager until the workflow is stable enough to trust.

That is the work I care about building for clients: not a flashy demo, but a managed digital employee with a narrow job, clear approval gates, and weekly improvement.

If you are evaluating AI agents, start with one workflow and one question:

What would this agent need to know, do, and escalate for us to trust it by Friday?
