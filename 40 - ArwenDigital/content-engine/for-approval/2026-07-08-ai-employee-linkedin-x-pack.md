### LinkedIn post text

```text
I do not think most small businesses should ask an AI agent to run a department on day one.

That sounds obvious until you watch the demos.

The agent reads the inbox, updates the CRM, schedules the meeting, drafts the proposal, sends the follow-up, summarizes the call, and somehow becomes a tiny operations team living in a browser.

It looks impressive.

It is also too much surface area for a first deployment.

A better starting point is smaller:

Where does work already get dropped between two steps?

That is usually the handoff.

Lead comes in → nobody follows up.
Call ends → notes never make it to the CRM.
Customer asks a question → the answer sits in someone’s inbox.
Report gets created → no one turns it into a decision.

Those are the places where an AI employee can be useful without becoming dangerous.

Give it one recurring handoff.
Give it clear inputs.
Give it a narrow output.
Give it an approval gate before anything customer-facing goes out.

That is less exciting than “autonomous department.”

But it is much more deployable.

The first win is not replacing a team.

The first win is making sure the work stops falling between people.
```

### LinkedIn post text

```text
Before you connect an AI agent to another tool, answer four permission questions.

What can it read?
What can it draft?
What can it change?
What can it send?

Most small-business AI failures do not start with the model suddenly becoming useless.

They start with fuzzy boundaries.

The agent can see too little, so every output is shallow.
Or it can see too much, so nobody trusts it.
It can draft work, but no one knows who approves it.
It can update a record, but the team cannot explain what changed.
It can send a message, but the owner is nervous every time it touches a customer.

That is not an AI problem only.

That is a management problem.

If you were hiring a human assistant, you would not say, “Here are all the keys. Just figure it out.”

You would define the job, the tools, the authority, and the escalation path.

AI employees need the same clarity.

Not because they are weak.

Because real businesses need trust before they need autonomy.
```

### LinkedIn post text

```text
The first AI employee should probably not be the most impressive one.

It should be the one your team can inspect.

That is the part a lot of AI-agent demos skip.

They show the agent doing ten things across five tools, then call it leverage.

But inside a real business, leverage is not enough.

The team needs to know:

What did it look at?
What did it decide?
What did it draft?
What changed?
Where did it stop?
Who approved the next step?

If nobody can answer those questions, the agent may still be “working,” but trust is leaking out of the system.

That is why boring workflows are underrated.

A weekly report.
A lead follow-up draft.
A CRM cleanup pass.
A call summary.
An inbox triage list.
A stale-deal reminder.

These are not flashy jobs.

But they happen often, have recognizable inputs, and create enough repetition for the agent to improve.

A good first AI employee should make one painful workflow more reliable.

Not make the whole company wonder what just happened.
```
