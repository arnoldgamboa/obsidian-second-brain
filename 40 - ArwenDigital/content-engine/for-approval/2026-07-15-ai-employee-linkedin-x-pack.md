### LinkedIn post text

```text
I do not think the next step for most AI agents is more permissions.

It is better visibility.

The demo version of AI agents usually makes access look like the unlock:

Connect the inbox.
Connect the CRM.
Connect the calendar.
Connect the proposal tool.
Let the agent move faster.

But inside a real small business, speed is not enough.

The owner still needs to know what happened.

What did the agent read?
What did it draft?
What did it change?
What did it skip?
Where did it stop and ask for help?

If nobody can answer those questions, giving the agent more access does not create trust. It creates a larger black box.

That is why I think the work log is underrated.

A useful AI employee should leave a trail. Not just a final output, but an inspectable record of the work:

- source used
- decision made
- draft created
- field changed
- message queued
- exception escalated

This is not bureaucracy.

It is how a business learns to trust delegated work.

Before expanding permissions, make the work visible.
```

### LinkedIn post text

```text
A good AI employee should not pretend every case is normal.

That is one of the places where agent demos can mislead small businesses.

The happy path looks clean:

A lead arrives.
The agent summarizes it.
The CRM gets updated.
A follow-up is drafted.
The next step is scheduled.

That is useful.

But operations are rarely just the happy path.

A customer asks something unusual.
A deal has missing context.
A record conflicts with another record.
A reply sounds angry.
A promise touches pricing, legal terms, delivery dates, or brand voice.

Those are not moments where the agent needs to be more confident.

Those are moments where the agent needs an exception path.

This is why I like starting AI agents with narrow workflows. You can define the normal lane and the escalation lane before the system touches too much of the business.

What should the agent handle automatically?
What should it draft but not send?
What should it summarize for a human?
What should it refuse to decide?

That is not slowing the agent down.

That is making the agent operationally useful.

Autonomy is only helpful when the business knows where autonomy stops.
```

### LinkedIn post text

```text
The most important question about an AI agent is not only, “Can it do the task?”

It is also, “Who improves it after week one?”

That is where a lot of AI adoption gets quietly stuck.

The first demo works well enough to feel exciting. The agent pulls data, drafts a response, updates a record, or summarizes a call.

Then real life starts.

The inputs are messier.
The team forgets to use the new process.
The edge cases show up.
The output is 80% right but not quite trusted.
The workflow changes because the business changes.

At that point, the agent does not just need a better prompt.

It needs management.

Someone has to review the work log, notice where the agent hesitated, tighten the instructions, adjust the approval gate, update the source of truth, and decide what the agent should own next.

That is the difference between buying an AI tool and operating an AI employee.

Tools get installed.

Employees get managed.

If you are evaluating AI agents for your business, do not only ask what the model can do today.

Ask who will keep improving the workflow next Friday.
```
