### LinkedIn post text

```text
Most businesses should not give an AI agent every key on day one.

The demos make it look simple:

Read the inbox.
Update the CRM.
Schedule the meeting.
Draft the proposal.
Send the follow-up.
Summarize the call.

That looks like leverage until you ask the question underneath the demo:

What is the agent actually allowed to touch?

This is where I think small businesses need less excitement and more operating discipline.

Before connecting an agent to a real workflow, write down four boundaries:

1. What can it read?
2. What can it draft?
3. What can it update?
4. What must wait for human approval?

That list is not bureaucracy.

It is how you make the agent safe enough for the team to use.

My bias is to start in read-and-draft mode. Let the agent summarize, prepare, compare, and recommend. Let the human approve anything that affects customers, money, legal commitments, or brand voice.

Then expand permissions after the workflow earns trust.

The best AI employee is not the one with the most access.

It is the one with the clearest job, the right boundaries, and a manager who knows when to promote it.
```

### LinkedIn post text

```text
A lot of AI agent conversations skip the manager.

They ask:

How autonomous can this be?
How many tools can it use?
How many steps can it run without a human?

Those are not bad questions. They are just not the first questions I would ask.

The better first question is:

Who is going to manage the agent while the workflow is still learning?

A new employee does not become useful because you give them unlimited freedom on day one. They become useful because someone gives them a clear job, shows them where the information lives, checks the first outputs, and explains what good work looks like.

AI agents are similar.

Unmanaged agents create hidden work:

- summaries that miss the real customer concern
- CRM updates nobody trusts
- follow-ups that sound almost right
- quiet assumptions because no escalation rule exists
- outputs that take longer to inspect than to write manually

That is not leverage.

That is operational debt with an automation costume.

A managed AI employee needs a weekly loop:

Define the job.
Limit the sources.
Review the output.
Fix the instruction.
Promote responsibility only after trust is earned.

Autonomy is not the starting point.

Management is.
```
