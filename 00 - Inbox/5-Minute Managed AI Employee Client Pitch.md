# Five-Minute Client Pitch: Managed AI Employees

**Objective:** Secure agreement for a focused workflow session, paid discovery, or managed pilot.

**Audience:** Busy business owners and executives whose attention is being consumed by recurring operational work.

**Core promise:** Remove one recurring responsibility from the client's plate without giving their team another technical system to operate.

## Hero message

> **Managed AI employees for busy business owners:**
>
> **You have better things to do than chase follow-ups, rebuild reports, and keep routine work moving.**

---

## 0:00–0:30 — Open with the outcome

> Thanks for giving me five minutes.
>
> You have better things to do than chase follow-ups, rebuild reports, and keep routine work moving.
>
> I design, build, and manage AI employees that take responsibility for clearly defined operational work, while your team keeps control of the decisions that require human judgment.

> The goal is simple: routine work gets handled, exceptions get surfaced, and your people spend more time on customers, decisions, and growth.

**Delivery note:** Do not begin with a long biography or an explanation of AI. Begin with the work the client wants off their plate.

---

## 0:30–1:05 — Make the problem feel expensive

> Most businesses do not have a shortage of software. They have a follow-through problem.
>
> Leads wait too long for a response. Follow-ups depend on someone remembering them. Reports get rebuilt every week. Important information sits across inboxes, spreadsheets, meeting notes, and different systems.
>
> Each task looks small. Together, they consume your team's attention and make growth harder than it needs to be.

Ask:

> **How much of that routine follow-through still finds its way back to you?**

Pause and allow the client to respond.

---

## 1:05–1:40 — Introduce the managed AI employee

> That is the work I solve.
>
> I build AI employees around clearly defined responsibilities inside your existing operations. They use approved business context, follow repeatable workflows, and bring decisions or exceptions to the right person.
>
> The difference is that I do not hand you a prototype and disappear. I connect the systems, establish approval points, monitor the work, maintain it, and improve it as your business changes.

### Memorable positioning

> **You manage the business outcome. I manage the AI employee.**

---

## 1:40–2:15 — Use the industry example closest to the client

Do not read every example during the pitch. Choose the one that most closely resembles the client's business.

### Professional services

Potential workflows:

- Qualify new inquiries and organize client intake
- Draft proposals from approved templates and meeting notes
- Capture commitments and prepare follow-up messages
- Update the CRM and surface opportunities that have gone quiet
- Assemble recurring client or management reports

### Real estate and property services

Potential workflows:

- Qualify buyer, seller, tenant, or investor inquiries
- Prepare personalized follow-ups after viewings
- Keep listings and CRM records current
- Track document requirements and outstanding actions
- Produce pipeline, occupancy, or property-management reports

### Clinics, dental practices, and wellness businesses

Potential workflows:

- Triage nonclinical inquiries
- Prepare appointment reminders and follow-ups
- Track incomplete forms and administrative requirements
- Summarize nonclinical communication for staff review
- Organize routine administrative reporting

**Guardrail:** Clinical decisions, diagnoses, and treatment advice remain with qualified people.

### E-commerce and retail

Potential workflows:

- Categorize support inquiries and prepare responses
- Track order exceptions, returns, and unresolved cases
- Request reviews after successful purchases
- Summarize recurring customer complaints
- Produce sales, inventory, and support reports

### Construction, home services, and trades

Potential workflows:

- Follow up on quote requests and estimates
- Prepare job-status updates for customers
- Track permits, documents, and outstanding actions
- Coordinate recurring scheduling communication
- Produce project and operations reports

### Agencies and marketing teams

Potential workflows:

- Coordinate client onboarding
- Capture action items from meetings
- Track content and campaign approvals
- Assemble recurring performance reports
- Follow up on overdue client inputs and project actions

### General transition line

> The industry changes, but the pattern is usually the same: information arrives, several routine actions should happen next, and someone currently has to remember and chase every step.

---

## 2:15–2:55 — Make it tangible with one workflow

### Lead-management example

> Imagine a new lead arrives through your website.
>
> The AI employee reviews the inquiry, researches the company, updates the CRM, prepares a personalized response, assigns the next action, and makes sure the opportunity is followed up.
>
> If the inquiry is routine, the process moves forward. If it requires judgment, pricing approval, or a sensitive response, the right person is notified with the necessary context.
>
> Your team does not have to remember every step. They receive the decisions and exceptions that actually require them.

### Alternative inbox example

> Imagine opening your inbox and finding routine messages categorized, responses prepared, commitments identified, and anything urgent waiting for your decision.

### Alternative reporting example

> Imagine your weekly report being assembled from the systems your team already uses, with missing information flagged before the report reaches you.

**Rule:** Present one workflow, not a catalogue of features. The client should be able to picture that responsibility leaving their plate.

---

## 2:55–3:30 — Turn the pitch into their opportunity

Ask:

> **If you could permanently stop chasing one recurring task in your business, what would you choose?**

Follow with:

> How often does it happen?

> Who handles it today?

> What happens when it is delayed or missed?

Then summarize:

> So the first opportunity is **[repeat the workflow]**. It happens **[frequency]**, currently requires **[people/time]**, and the main cost is **[delay, missed opportunity, administrative time, or inconsistency]**.

---

## 3:30–4:15 — Explain the managed engagement and avoided technical burden

> I would not begin by trying to automate the whole business.
>
> I would start with **[workflow]**, define exactly what the AI employee owns, establish where human approval is required, connect the necessary systems, and agree on how we will measure success.
>
> I would then build it, test it with your team, put it into operation, and continue managing it for you.

### What the engagement includes

- Workflow analysis and solution design
- Custom implementation
- Connections to approved business systems
- Human approval and escalation rules
- Hosting and deployment
- Monitoring, maintenance, and troubleshooting
- Ongoing workflow improvement
- Team onboarding and support

### Technical costs and responsibilities the client does not have to assemble

Within the agreed managed scope, Arnold selects, administers, pays for or includes, monitors, and maintains the technical components needed by the AI employee. The client does not have to independently assemble and operate:

1. **AI model access and usage** — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, or other approved model providers
2. **Automation and orchestration platforms** — workflow engines, schedulers, queues, and related tooling
3. **Cloud hosting and compute** — servers, containers, functions, and runtime infrastructure
4. **Databases and storage** — workflow state, files, search indexes, and approved business context
5. **Monitoring, logging, and alerts** — health checks, error reporting, and workflow visibility
6. **Backups and recovery procedures** — backups for managed components and recovery planning
7. **Security maintenance** — permissions, secrets management, patches, and dependency updates
8. **Deployment and version upgrades** — releases, environment configuration, and technical upgrades
9. **API and integration maintenance** — repairs when connected services change their APIs or authentication
10. **Model evaluation and switching** — choosing the right model and changing providers when appropriate
11. **Prompt and workflow optimization** — improving instructions, context, routing, and outputs
12. **Testing and quality checks** — workflow tests, approval checks, and regression testing
13. **Debugging and incident response** — investigating failures and restoring managed workflows
14. **Technical documentation** — keeping the managed system understandable and maintainable
15. **Ongoing technical support** — answering questions and improving the system as the business changes

### Important scope clarification

> The proposal will state exactly what is included, any usage limits, and which external costs remain the client's responsibility.

The client's existing business software, such as its CRM, accounting platform, email system, or industry-specific application, normally remains the client's subscription unless the proposal explicitly includes it. High-volume usage, premium third-party data, messaging fees, or unusual infrastructure requirements may also be priced separately.

### Cost-positioning line

> **You are not buying a collection of AI subscriptions. You are buying a managed business outcome.**

---

## 4:15–5:00 — Present the investment and close

### Pricing transition

> Managed engagements **start at $3,000 per month**, depending on the workflow, model usage, integrations, and support required.
>
> Before anything begins, I will give you a clear scope showing what is included, any usage limits, the implementation plan, and the outcome we will measure.

> The right comparison is not one software subscription. It is the total cost of the technical stack, implementation, maintenance, staff time, delays, missed follow-ups, and management attention.

### Recommended close

> From what you have told me, **[workflow]** appears to be the right place to start.
>
> The next step is a focused workflow session where we map the responsibility, systems, safeguards, and expected outcome. After that, I will give you a clear scope, timeline, and proposal.

> **Would you like to move forward with that session?**

Then stop talking.

If they say yes:

> **Excellent. Would Tuesday or Thursday work better?**

Secure the date while you are together.

# Responses to common objections

## We already use ChatGPT or another AI product

> That is useful, but it still depends on someone prompting it, supplying context, checking the work, and remembering what happens next. I build and manage the repeatable operating system around the AI so the responsibility keeps moving.

## Why not subscribe to these tools ourselves?

> You could, but then your team becomes responsible for selecting the tools, connecting them, securing them, monitoring them, repairing them, and improving them. My service gives you the working outcome without turning your team into an AI operations department.

## We need to think about it

> Of course. What specifically do you need to feel confident about: the workflow, security, implementation, scope, or investment?

## We are concerned about security

> That should be addressed before implementation. We define what the AI employee can access, what it cannot access, where approval is required, how activity is logged, and how credentials are protected. Access is limited to what the workflow needs.

## It sounds expensive

> That is fair. We should compare the investment against the complete cost of the technology, implementation, maintenance, staff time, delays, missed follow-ups, and management attention. If the return is unclear, I will recommend narrowing the scope or not building it.

## Can we start smaller?

> Yes. Starting with one well-defined workflow is usually the best approach. It limits risk, gives us a visible result, and creates a foundation we expand only if it proves useful.

## Will this replace our employees?

> The objective is to remove repetitive operational work, not indiscriminately remove people. Your team keeps the judgment, relationships, and decisions. The AI employee handles the routine execution surrounding them.
