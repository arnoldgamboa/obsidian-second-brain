# Five-Minute Client Pitch: Managed AI Employees

**Objective:** Secure agreement for a paid workflow discovery session or managed pilot.

**Audience:** Business owners and executives overwhelmed by operational work.

**Core promise:** Remove one recurring responsibility from the client's plate without giving their team another system to manage.

---

## 0:00–0:30 — Open with the outcome

> Thanks for giving me five minutes. I'm not here to sell you another AI subscription. I want to show you how we can take one recurring responsibility off your team's plate and keep it moving without someone constantly chasing it.

> The goal is simple: routine work gets handled, exceptions get surfaced, and your people spend more time on customers, decisions, and growth.

**Delivery note:** Do not introduce yourself with a long biography. Start with what changes for the client.

---

## 0:30–1:15 — Make the problem feel expensive

> Most businesses don't have a shortage of tools. They have a follow-through problem.

> Leads wait too long for a response. Follow-ups depend on someone remembering them. Reports get rebuilt every week. Important information sits across inboxes, spreadsheets, meeting notes, and different systems.

> Each task looks small. Together, they consume your team's attention and make growth more difficult than it needs to be.

Then ask:

> **How much of that routine follow-through still finds its way back to you?**

Pause and let the client answer.

---

## 1:15–2:00 — Introduce the managed AI employee

> That is the work I solve.

> I design, build, and manage AI employees around clearly defined responsibilities inside your existing operations.

> An AI employee might triage an inbox, prepare responses, follow up with leads, update the CRM, produce recurring reports, research opportunities, or track commitments before they get forgotten.

> The difference is that I don't hand you a prototype and disappear. I connect the systems, establish approval points, monitor the work, maintain it, and improve it as your business changes.

### Memorable positioning

> **You manage the business outcome. I manage the AI employee.**

---

## 2:00–3:00 — Make it tangible with one workflow

Choose one example that matches the client.

### Lead-management example

> Imagine a new lead arrives through your website.

> The AI employee reviews the inquiry, researches the company, updates the CRM, prepares a personalized response, assigns the next action, and makes sure the opportunity is followed up.

> If the inquiry is routine, the process moves forward. If it needs judgment, pricing approval, or a sensitive response, the right person is notified with the necessary context.

> Your team doesn't have to remember every step. They receive the decisions and exceptions that actually require them.

### Alternative inbox example

> Imagine opening your inbox and finding that routine messages have been categorized, responses are already prepared, commitments have been identified, and anything urgent is waiting for your decision.

### Alternative reporting example

> Imagine your weekly report being assembled automatically from the systems your team already uses, with missing information flagged before the report reaches you.

**Rule:** Present only one example. The client should be able to picture it working in their business.

---

## 3:00–3:40 — Turn the pitch into their opportunity

Ask:

> **If you could permanently stop chasing one recurring task in your business, what would you choose?**

Follow with:

> How often does that happen?

> Who currently handles it?

> What happens when it is delayed or missed?

Then summarize:

> So the first opportunity is **[repeat the workflow]**. It happens **[frequency]**, currently requires **[people/time]**, and the main cost is **[delay, missed opportunity, administrative time, or inconsistency]**.

This makes the client feel understood and gives you a concrete workflow to sell.

---

## 3:40–4:25 — Present the engagement

> Based on what you've described, I would not begin by trying to automate the whole business.

> I would start with **[workflow]**, define exactly what the AI employee is responsible for, identify where human approval is needed, connect the necessary systems, and establish how we will measure whether it is working.

> I would then build it, test it with your team, put it into operation, and continue managing it for you.

### Scope statement

The engagement includes:

- Workflow analysis
- Custom design and implementation
- Connections to approved business systems
- Human approval and escalation rules
- Hosting and monitoring
- Maintenance and troubleshooting
- Ongoing improvement
- Team training and support

### Pricing transition

If you are using the current price anchor:

> A typical managed engagement is around **$4,000 per month**, depending on the workflow, integrations, and support required. We confirm the final scope and price before anything begins.

Do not apologize for the price or immediately discount it.

---

## 4:25–5:00 — Close decisively

### Recommended close

> From what you've told me, **[workflow]** appears to be the right place to start.

> The next step is a focused workflow session where we map the responsibility, systems, safeguards, and expected outcome. After that, I'll give you a clear scope, timeline, and proposal.

> **Would you like to move forward with that session?**

Then stop talking.

If they say yes:

> **Excellent. Would Tuesday or Thursday work better?**

Secure the date while you are together.

---

# Stronger direct close

Use this when the client already understands the service, has authority, and appears ready:

> If we agree that **[workflow]** is the right starting point and the final scope fits the budget we discussed, is there anything preventing us from getting started?

If they say no:

> Great. Let's reserve the workflow session and establish a target start date.

# Responses to common objections

## We already use ChatGPT or another AI product

> That is useful, but it still depends on someone prompting it, supplying context, checking the work, and remembering what happens next. I build the repeatable operating system around the AI so the responsibility keeps moving without manual prompting every time.

## We need to think about it

> Of course. What specifically do you need to feel confident about: the workflow, security, implementation, or investment?

Address the actual concern instead of ending the conversation vaguely.

## We're concerned about security

> That should be addressed before implementation. We define what the AI employee can access, what it cannot access, where approval is required, and how activity is logged. Access is limited to what the workflow needs.

## It sounds expensive

> That is fair. We should compare the investment against what this responsibility currently costs in staff time, delays, missed follow-ups, and management attention. If the return is unclear, I will recommend narrowing the scope or not building it.

## Can we start smaller?

> Yes. Starting with one well-defined workflow is usually the best approach. It gives us a visible result, limits risk, and creates a foundation we can expand only if it proves useful.

## Will this replace our employees?

> The objective is to remove repetitive operational work, not indiscriminately remove people. Your team keeps the judgment, relationships, and decisions. The AI employee handles the routine execution surrounding them.
